Partial Derivatives

Understanding how functions change with respect to one variable while keeping others constant

Concept Overview

A partial derivative of a function of several variables is its derivative with respect to one of those variables, with the others held constant. Partial derivatives are used in vector calculus and differential geometry.

Mathematically:

For a function \( f(x,y) \), the partial derivative with respect to \( x \) is: \[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h,y) - f(x,y)}{h} \]

In physics, partial derivatives appear in thermodynamics (where we hold certain variables constant), electromagnetism (Maxwell's equations), quantum mechanics (Schr√∂dinger equation), and many other areas.

Physics Application: Thermodynamics

In thermodynamics, partial derivatives are essential for understanding how state variables relate to each other.

Example: Heat Capacity

The heat capacity at constant volume is: \[ C_V = \left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial T} \right)_V \] where \( U \) is internal energy, \( T \) is temperature, and the subscript \( V \) indicates volume is held constant.

Worked Example

Problem: The pressure \( P \), volume \( V \), and temperature \( T \) of an ideal gas are related by the equation \( PV = nRT \), where \( n \) is the amount of substance and \( R \) is the ideal gas constant. Find \( \left( \frac{\partial P}{\partial T} \right)_V \) and interpret its physical meaning.

From the ideal gas law: \[ P = \frac{nRT}{V} \]

Differentiating both sides with respect to \( T \) while treating \( V \) as constant: \[ \left( \frac{\partial P}{\partial T} \right)_V = \frac{\partial}{\partial T} \left( \frac{nRT}{V} \right) = \frac{nR}{V} \]

The result \( \frac{nR}{V} \) represents how much the pressure changes per unit temperature increase when the volume is held constant. This is directly proportional to the amount of gas and inversely proportional to the volume.

Practice Problems

Problem 1

For the function \( f(x,y) = x^2 y + y^3 \), find \( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \) and \( \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \).

Remember that when taking partial derivatives with respect to one variable, treat all other variables as constants.

\( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 2xy \)
\( \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = x^2 + 3y^2 \)

Problem 2

In thermodynamics, the enthalpy \( H \) is defined as \( H = U + PV \), where \( U \) is internal energy, \( P \) is pressure, and \( V \) is volume. Find an expression for the heat capacity at constant pressure \( C_P = \left( \frac{\partial H}{\partial T} \right)_P \) in terms of partial derivatives of \( U \).

Use the product rule when differentiating \( PV \), and remember that \( P \) is held constant in this partial derivative.

\( C_P = \left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial T} \right)_P + P \left( \frac{\partial V}{\partial T} \right)_P \)
